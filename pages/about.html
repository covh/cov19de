Summary:

Study per district ("Kreis", "Landkreis"), the number of reported infections, in two ways:
  1. in comparison to other regions, via a variation of the conditional formatting table of Tomas Pueyo in his Coronavirus: Learning How to Dance --> chart 3
  2. as a time series plot of observables over time (cumulative total cases, daily new cases, smoothed with a simple moving average)

All is based on the excellent crowdsourced data by RiskLayer.com, a research group in Karlsruhe.

Assumptions:

(A1) averaging of the raw data

Probably due to less data reporting work on weekends, the data quality in Germany has one clear flaw: It fluctuates in a weekly rhythm (best to see e.g. in the GRAY wavy curve in the Germany plot). As that mostly delays the reporting (even though in mild cases it might also lead to some unreported cases?), the total number of cases x-days-later will not be (much) affected by that. But the momentary situation "today" or "yesterday" is quite unclear. One workaround to minimize that disturbance comes in two steps: (1) averaging of the cumulative total number of cases over the past 7 days = add up all 7 values, and divide by 7.0, and then (2) shifting that result to the left, by 3 days, because that is where the "center" of that 7 days average is sitting. For this step (1) "averaging" there are actually many choices how to do it, see e.g. this wikipedia page - for now we are choosing a central "simple moving average".

(A1.1) where averages are used

  • plots: in most plots you can see that the 7 day rhythm is well smoothed already, no real need for a 14 days window, compare: 7 days SMA (purple) and 14 days SMA (orange).
  • tables: the background colors of the table cells: first the cumulative total cases is smoothed, with 7-days-window, and centered; then a "synthetic daily new cases" is calculated from that, and used as the coloring scalar between 0 and the max occuring number in that row.

(A1.2) where no averages are used

  • plots: the GRAY wavy line is the (real, reported, raw, unaveraged) daily number of new cases
  • tables: the numbers themselves, in the colored table cells, are the (real, reported, raw, unaveraged) total number of cases until that day.
  • the following "center day", see (A2), is calculated from the (real, raw, reported, unaveraged) input data of total cases, by taking a difference of two consecutive total cases, to get the daily_cases(daynumber) which is the input sequence for:

(A2) sorting by 'center day'

This is used in two places --> The GREEN bar in each plot marks that day. And all comparison heatmap tables are sorted by that number, shown in the 'center day' column.
What is it? -->  (At least until 2nd waves are happening ...) a good proxy for how relatively dramatic the situation still is in a certain region, is what we call the "center day" of the daily new cases. The longer ago that center day, the more likely the outbreak is under control now.  The center day is calculated like this:

     centerday = sum_over_all_daynumbers [ daynumber * daily_cases(daynumber) ] / total_cases

with
total_cases = sum_over_all_daynumbers [ daily_cases(daynumber) ]
daynumber = 0 is the first day for which we have data (and incrementing for each later day), and
daily(daynumber) = the number of additional cases per daynumber (note that for the very first day (the day with daynumber = 0) that is undefined.)

in other words, the "center day" is: the average day, weighted with the number of new cases for each day.

(A3) sorting by 'anything'

Now all tables can be SORTED by a specific column, when clicking the column title text (Note that the large table can take 30 seconds to be sorted. Please be patient. The yellow color will disappear when the sorting is done). Enable Javascript for this work.
